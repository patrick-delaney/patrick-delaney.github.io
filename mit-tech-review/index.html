It's easy to make a boring blog. It's a lot more fun evolve a blog into an industry destination and get nominated for a Webby while you do it.

Client

Founded in 1899(!), MIT Technology Review is a bimonthly print and digital magazine owned by, but independent of, the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

With a passion for tech and an eye to the future, MITTR wanted their digital presence to be as cutting edge as their stories.

Challenge

Evolve Tech Review's homepage from a linear feed into a flexible content hub without adding complexity or upending their editorial model.

Goals

  • Provide ability to curate content in unique and exciting ways via CMS.
  • Clarify CTA strategy, increase user clickthrough on core KPIs.
  • Tie the site together with a UX-driven digital brand refresh.

So, how did we do it?

Tech Review came to us with a design strategy that worked, but was reaching its limits without a clear plan for the future.

With few options for variety or curation, users didn't have a enough incentive to scroll or subscribe. The content was great, we just needed to advocate it.

The new blueprint

Using Tech Review's current content as a guide, we rebuilt their homepage using a modular component system that opened up a world of possibilities.

Everyone loves legos

The new visual system needed to be bold, modular, yet cohesive. Our solution was 'LEGO blocks' of content that lived independently of each other but snapped together to form a confident whole.

Hero

The first thing users see. Clear two-column structure with ability to swap color schemes and content on a daily basis.

Newsletter

A visual anchor high on the page, designed to generate interest and provide a clear CTA regardless of specific newsletter highlighted.

Story Groups

A flexible, dynamic grouping of stories with several distinct arrangements. Group could be hand-curated, auto-generated, or timed to magazine drop.

...and more!

We wanted it to be easy to add new modules, ad blocks, and 3rd-party integrations without breaking the rest of the page.

Hit the griddy

These layouts looked cool, but could easily become a nightmare to maintain. To keep things simple, we introduced a grid system called the “ice cube tray,”designed to appear complex and tailored but be simple and lightweight under the hood.

Paste the rainbow

A canvas is no good if you have nothing to paint with. In addition to the new grid, we introduced a modular palette with over 100 accessible, harmonious color combinations.

Highlights

Chalky and bright, reserved for text and visual accents. Always live on “top” of the backgrounds, should command attention and imply interactivity.

Tones

Rich and subtle, strictly used as a background element to unite large, related groups of content.

Combinations

By keeping highlights and tones separate, we could ensure all color combos were visually pleasing and accessible, even if you picked them at random.

Guidelines

The color palette was designed to elevate Tech Review's content and not force a single look or feel. Combinations could be quirky and cheerful or sober and reserved. It was important that there be no wrong choices, only contextual options.

Method to the radness

Of course, at the end of the day, this still has to be built. For engineering handoff, we created a Figma library that structured designs at the atomic level.

Framework

Atomic elements used to build sitewide componentry. Think eyebrows, lists, breadcrumbs, etc.

Modules

Modular, distinct lego bricks of content. Built with atomic components but with flexibility for one-off elements.

Layouts

Modular, distinct lego bricks of content. Built with atomic components but with flexibility for one-off elements.

Pages

Final designs composed of multiple layouts stacked on top of one another.
